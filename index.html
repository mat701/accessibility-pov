The P.O.V. Framework

Proximity · Opportunity · Value

This interactive visualisation accompanies the research paper The dimensions of accessibility: proximity, opportunities, values by Bruno, Campanelli, Melo, Rossi Mori & Loreto (EPJ Data Science, 2026).

What are Proximity and Opportunity?

Accessibility is not one-dimensional. The framework separates it into two spatial scales:

Proximity measures access to everyday services — grocery stores, pharmacies, cafés, schools — reachable on foot within short distances. It captures the richness of local neighbourhood life: how much you can do without leaving your area.
Opportunity measures access to city-scale resources — jobs, universities, stadiums, cultural venues — reachable via public transport within reasonable travel times. It captures how well a neighbourhood is connected to the broader city.

The four zones

Each hexagonal cell in a city is classified based on whether its Proximity and Opportunity scores fall above or below the city-wide median:

Inclusion
Above-median in both dimensions. Well-served locally and well-connected globally.
Spatial Isolation
Good local services but poor transit connections. A spatial mismatch — the neighbourhood is lively but disconnected from distant opportunities like specialised jobs.
Social Isolation
Good transit but few local amenities. You can reach the city centre quickly, but lack a walkable neighbourhood with everyday services and community life.
Total Isolation
Below-median in both dimensions. Poorly served locally and poorly connected globally — a car is likely essential.

The third dimension: Value

The paper also theorises a third axis — Value — capturing the quality and desirability of reachable services. Two neighbourhoods may have similar proximity scores, but differ vastly in the quality of their schools, restaurants, or healthcare. Value remains difficult to quantify and is not yet measured in this tool, but it is a critical dimension for understanding real-world accessibility.

How to read the visualisation

The cartogram (left panel) shows the city divided into ~200 m hexagonal cells, coloured by their zone type. The scatter plot (right panel) places each hexagon by its Opportunity (x-axis) and Proximity (y-axis) scores, with dashed lines marking the city-wide medians. Click any hexagon on either panel to see its details.

Data & methods

Cities are covered with H3 resolution-9 hexagons (~200 m sides). Walking times are computed via OSRM on OpenStreetMap road networks. Public transport travel times use GTFS schedules combined with a Connection Scan Algorithm. Points of Interest from OpenStreetMap serve as opportunity proxies. Population data comes from WorldPop, adjusted to UN estimates.

Reference: Bruno M., Campanelli B., Melo H.P.M., Rossi Mori L., Loreto V. (2026). The dimensions of accessibility: proximity, opportunities, values. EPJ Data Science, 15:22. doi:10.1140/epjds/s13688-026-00623-8

Reading the scatter plot

Each dot is a hexagonal cell in the city. Its position encodes:

  • X-axis (Opportunity): how many points of interest are reachable by public transport, weighted by travel time.
  • Y-axis (Proximity): how many points of interest are reachable on foot, weighted by walking time.

The dashed lines mark the city-wide median values. Dots are coloured by zone type. Hover or click to cross-highlight on the map.

The cluster of points in the bottom-left corner often consists of hexagons from which no public transport stops can be accessed within 15 minutes of walking.

Zone type

Inclusion
High proximity + High opportunity. This area has rich local services and good public transport connections to the rest of the city. Residents can walk to everyday needs and easily reach distant jobs, cultural venues, and universities.
Spatial Isolation
High proximity + Low opportunity. The neighbourhood has shops, cafés, and schools nearby, but poor transit links to the wider city. Reaching a specialised job or a major cultural venue is difficult without a car.
Social Isolation
Low proximity + High opportunity. Well connected by transit to the city centre, but the local neighbourhood lacks everyday services — few shops, restaurants, or community spaces within walking distance.
Total Isolation
Low proximity + Low opportunity. Neither local services nor good transit connections. A car is likely essential for daily life. These areas are typically found at the urban periphery.
